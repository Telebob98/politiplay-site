How To Play
Everything you need to draft your first squad, score your first Sweep, and beat your table. No fantasy sports experience required.
Section 1

The Concept

You already have opinions about politics. You read the news. You argue with people. You think you know where things are headed. Politiplay gives your instincts a score.

Every week you draft a squad of ten real political figures — actual heads of state, legislators, opposition leaders, world bodies, and the sidelined-but-dangerous figures known as Kibitzers and Criers. When your figures drive the global news cycle, your squad earns points. At the end of the week, the player at your table with the most points wins.

No dice. No luck of the draw. The news decides. The player who best anticipated where the political winds were blowing takes the table.

Section 2 — Start here

Your Table

You don't compete against the entire Politiplay player base. You compete at a table of ten — nine other players who think they read the room just as well as you do. Your weekly ranking is your position at that table, not a global leaderboard.

This matters because the table is where the game actually lives. The rivalry is personal. The trash talk is real. When your Kibitzer pick explodes for 22 points and nobody else at your table saw it coming — you'll feel it.

Your seat

Your squad is private before the Sweep opens. Nobody can see what you drafted until scoring begins. Once the Sweep is live, all ten squads are fully visible to everyone at your table. You can see exactly who your opponents drafted — and they can see you.

Each table also has a chat window. Argue your picks. Call out your rivals. The table is the social unit. The game is just the reason you're all in the same room.

Section 4 — The volatile slot

Kibitzers & Criers

Kibitzers once had the room — or almost did. They know where the bodies are buried because they helped bury some of them. Former presidents, deposed leaders, exiled politicians, figures of former prominence who can't stop commenting on what's happening inside the room they used to occupy.

When a Kibitzer surfaces, it matters. A leaked document, a court date, a book, a Senate hearing that names them. These are resurgence events — the past becoming suddenly relevant again.

Criers never had the room, and won't stop saying so. The activists, the independent media voices, the bloggers, the figures of uncertain provenance who amplify, agitate, and occasionally detonate. Their power is attention, not position.

A Crier can go from irrelevant to unavoidable overnight. Ghislaine Maxwell names names in a court filing. Alex Jones says something that breaks containment. A blogger turns out to be right about everything. That's 18 points nobody saw coming.

The 1.5× multiplier applies to both — the game doesn't distinguish. You decide which one you're drafting. The news decides which one matters this week. That's the Wildcard bet.

Section 6

How Scoring Works

Points are awarded when your figures drive the news cycle. Events are detected automatically via GDELT and NewsAPI news monitoring — then reviewed, verified, and logged by the editorial team. Every scoring event has a source, a headline, and a direct link. Players can verify every point.

Scoring ranges by event type: major executive actions score 12–16 points, legislative floor moments 8–12, international crisis events 10–14, WBR visibility events 14–22. A figure can score multiple times in a single Sweep — once for each qualifying event.

The ownership rule: if multiple players at your table draft the same figure, that figure's point value is diluted for all of them. The more people who own a figure at your table, the less it's worth to each of them. The game rewards the pick others missed.

Section 7

The Swap

You get one swap per Sweep. Use it before Friday at midnight. After that your roster is fully locked until the Sweep closes Sunday night.

The swap is your one correction — use it on knowledge you had at draft time but didn't fully act on, not as a reaction to events already in motion. Swapping in a figure because they just made news isn't prediction. It's arbitrage. The game is better than that.

Section 8 — Coming Week 4

The Court of Public Opinion

Not every news event is just a headline. Some events carry moral weight the news cycle score alone doesn't capture. Each week the scoring team flags the most consequential moments in red letter ink.

These flagged events go to the Court of Public Opinion after the Sweep closes. Your table votes on their true point value — positive or negative, on a scale of −20 to +20. The table's aggregate verdict is recorded in a separate moral ledger that runs alongside the game score.

Your Court record accumulates across the season. Over time it answers a different question than the leaderboard: not just whether you predicted the news cycle correctly — but whether you understood what it meant.
